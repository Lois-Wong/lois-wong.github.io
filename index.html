As the first AI Librarian at the University of Chicago, I work on AI literacy and adoption at an institutional level. I manage a strategic priority on AI, teach student and faculty workshops, and build risk evaluation frameworks for assessing internal projects and vendor tools.

I have a BA in Linguistics from UC Berkeley and an MSE in Computer Science from Johns Hopkins University. This combination allows me to communicate effectively with technical and non-technical stakeholders and design how AI should be taught. Experiences across big tech, startups, nonprofits, and academia have given me a strong understanding how novel technologies like AI are built, integrated, and received in a variety of contexts and priorities.

What I'm developing: an open and modular AI education program accessible to learners without traditional technical backgrounds. Unlike existing AI education resources, this prioritizes civic participation, critique, and responsible use over technical career placement.

AI literacy is becoming a gatekeeping competency. Without it, people are excluded from professional opportunity, and more importantly, from participating in decisions about systems that impact everyday life. Addressing bias and harm requires contributions from different academic fields and the communities affected by these technologies, and current AI education resources do not support these learners.

Prequel: Gaita is a conversational RAG system that generates personalized Computer Science learning pathways from open-access courseware. Inspired by my own transition into tech, Gaita was designed to make CS education accessible to learners from non-technical and non-traditional backgrounds.

Research interests: How archival silence and unrepresentative training data surface as algorithmic bias and model error, as well as developing metrics, methods, and workflows to identify and mitigate these issues. Language models learn from data that disproportionately encode the ideas and perspectives of privileged groups, e.g. those historically afforded an education and platform, while underrepresenting others due to factors like data quality, language, and uneven digitization.
